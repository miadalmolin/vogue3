    1920s

    The 1920s, or the Roaring 20, was a time of rapid change and chaos. During this time, Coco Chanel created something that would revolutionise womenswear and fashion forever. The Little Black Dress, or LBD, was a term coined by Vogue based on the dress Coco Chanel created. At a time where vibrant and elaborate flapper dresses reigned, Coco Chanel's audacious move was groundbreaking and never-before-seen. In 1926, she presented a long-sleeved black mini dress, challenging the convention that associated black dresses with mourning and servitude.

    This black dress would be named by Vogue as 'Chanel's Ford'. It was simple and accessible for women of all social classes. With its understated charm, the LBD challenged societal norms, paving the way for a fashion revolution that transcended time. This sleek, versatile garment would swiftly become a symbol of elegance and modernity over the next decade...

    1930s

    The 1930s embraced the LBD wholeheartedly, integrating it into mainstream fashion. It represented the changing roles of women, embracing newfound feedom and independence. The Great Depression dominated the 1930s. During this era of economic turmoil, the LBD became a beacon of style and affordability.

    The tasseled flapper dress, a variation of the LBD, symbolised female liberation. Its playful fringe and effortless charm captured the essence of newfound freedom in fashion. Women embraced the LBD as a symbol of empowerment, wearing it with confidence and grace. This garment not only provided an escape from the harsh realities of the Great Depression, but also represented resilience and sophistication, making a lasting mark on fashion for the future.

    in technicolour

    in technicolour

    1940s

    In the forties, the Little Black Dress underwent a transformation, becoming mroe accessible and popular than ever before. With the increased availability of synthetic fabrics, the LBD became affordable for a wider audience, allowing women from all social backgrounds to indulge in its timeless allure.

    During this decade, Hollywood played a major role in shaping the LBD's image. The black hue of the dress translated beautifully on the first Technicolour screens, enhancing its elegance and mystique. At this point, the LBD was becoming a wardrobe staple, symbolising both practicality and glamour simultaneously - this was a new and exciting concept! As women entered the workforce during WWII, the LBD offered a sense of confidence, becoming a symbol of resilience in challenging times.

    1950s

    a new look?

    a new look?

    1950s

    The 1950s ushered in an era of post-war conservatism, but the Little Black Dress retained its rebellious spirit. While Dior's "New Look" influenced housewives to embrace a more conservative style, the LBD persisted as a symbol of defiance. In light of this, the LBD also took on a 'new look', of sorts - featuring a cinched waist and hourglass silhouette, it became the embodiment of the "dangerous woman".

    Hollywood femme fatales flaunted this provocative style, challenging the conservative societal norms at the time with its alluring charm. The LBD, with its bold yet graceful design, allowed women to express confidence and sensuality, making a statement in an otherwise conformist society. It persisted as a symbol of female empowerment, breaking free from the constraints of the past and embracing a bold, new identity.

    1960s

    Audrey's reign

    Audrey's reign

    1960s

    The swinging sixties brought a wave of change, reflecting the youth's rebellion against traditional norms. The lBD, too, underwent another radical transformation during this period. Mod-style black mini dresses became a staple among the younger generation, embracing the spirit of freedom and self-expression. These playful, short dresses embodied the youthful exuberance of the era, embracing bold patterns and daring cuts.

    Simultaneously, the elegant sheath dress also gained popularity, epitomised by the iconic black Givenchy dress worn by the one and only Audrey Hepburn in Breakfast at Tiffany's. This modest, yet alluring style became the preferred choice for the more mature generation, embodying sophistication and timeless beauty. The LBD, in its various forms, became a symbol of individuality, capturing the essence of a generation in flux.

    the

    1970s

    embellishments galore

    embellishments galore

    1970s

    Disco baby! The disco era of the 1970's infused the Little Black Dress with a newfound sparkle. Influenced by the glitz and glamour of discotheques, LBDs were seen adorned with sequins, beads, and sparkly embellishments, capturing the spirit of the dancefloor. These shimmering garments became synonymous with dazzling nightlife, embodying the carefree energy of the disco era.

    Later in the decade, the LBD did a 180 and embraced the rebellious spirit of punk rock culture. Leather, PVC, safety pins and fishnets transformed the LBD into a punk symbol which challenged the traditional norms of femininity, championing non-conformity and a do-it-yourself ethic. The LBD became a canvas for self-expression, reflecting the diverse and evolving cultural landscape of the 70s.

    1980s

    going all out

    going all out

    1980s

    The 80s, marked by bold fashion choices and dramatic silouettes, transformed the LBD into a powerful statement piece. Casual fabrics and knits gained prominence, offering comfort without compromising style. The LBD embraced the era's preference for exaggerated shapes, featuring broad shoulders and peplum styles.

    These bold designs exuded confidence and sophistication, making a big fashion statement. The LBD reflected the assertive spirit of the 1980s. It was no longer just a garment but a manifestation of boldness and individuality, capturing the essence of the era's larger-than-life fashion trends.

    the

    1990s

    make it yours

    make it yours

    1990s

    The nineties witnessed yet another shift in the style of the Little Black Dress, in stark contrast to the decade previous. It embraced both minimalism, and edginess. The popularity of the simple slip dress redefined the LBD yet again, offering a sleek and effortless look. This minimalist approach to fashion became emblematic of the decade's understated elegance.

    Simultaneously, the LBD embraced grunge culture, taking on an edgier persona. Paired with combat boots and sandals, the LBD yet again was a symbol of rebellion and challenging traditional notions of femininity. This swicth also highlights a 20-year trend cycle, with trends from the 70s revisited 20 years later. The 90s LBD was versatile, adapting to the diverse fashion preferences of the era, making a statement both on the runway and the streets.

    the

    2000s

    a wardrobe staple

    a wardrobe staple

    2000s

    The turn of the century marked a period of nostalgic revival for the Little Black Dress. Revisiting styles from earlier decades, the LBD embraced a timeless appeal, combining the best elements of its predecessors. Its versatility became its defining feature, adapting effortlessly to changing trends and fashion preferences. From beach days to red carpet events, the LBD became a go-to choice for celebrities and the general public alike. Its enduring popularity made it a staple in every womans wardrobe, transcending age and occasion.

    The LBD remains a symbol of timeless elegance, embodying the essence of classic fashion while embracing the spirit of contemporary trends. It is a canvas for creativity, allowing designers and fashion enthusiasts (or anyone!) to reinterpret its iconic silhouette, ensuring its relevance in the ever-changing world of fashion.
